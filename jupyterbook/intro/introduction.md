# Introduction

## Background on Social Security Benefits Taxation

Social Security benefits taxation was first introduced in 1983 as part of the Social Security Amendments Act, marking a significant shift in how the federal government treated retirement income. The taxation of Social Security benefits serves dual purposes: generating additional revenue for the federal government and ensuring that higher-income beneficiaries contribute more to the system's financing.

### Current Law Framework

Under current law, Social Security benefits are subject to federal income tax based on a taxpayer's combined income, which includes:

- Adjusted Gross Income (AGI)
- Tax-exempt interest income  
- One-half of Social Security benefits

The taxation follows a two-tier structure:

**Tier 1 (Base Taxation):**
- For single filers: 50% of benefits are taxable if combined income exceeds $25,000
- For joint filers: 50% of benefits are taxable if combined income exceeds $32,000

**Tier 2 (Additional Taxation):**
- For single filers: 85% of benefits are taxable if combined income exceeds $34,000
- For joint filers: 85% of benefits are taxable if combined income exceeds $44,000

### Revenue Allocation

Revenue from Social Security benefits taxation is allocated to the Social Security trust funds:
- **OASI (Old-Age and Survivors Insurance)**: Receives approximately 85% of the revenue
- **DI (Disability Insurance)**: Receives approximately 15% of the revenue

This allocation reflects the proportional share of benefits paid by each trust fund.

### The Bonus Senior Deduction

The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 introduced a temporary "bonus senior deduction" that allows taxpayers aged 65 and older to claim an additional standard deduction of $1,300 (single) or $1,600 (married filing jointly). This provision is scheduled to expire at the end of 2028, which creates additional complexity for Social Security taxation reform proposals.

### Policy Context and Motivation

The current Social Security taxation system faces several challenges:

1. **Complexity**: The two-tier structure with different thresholds creates administrative complexity for taxpayers and the IRS
2. **Horizontal Equity**: Similar-income taxpayers may face different effective tax rates depending on their income composition
3. **Trust Fund Solvency**: The Social Security trust funds face long-term solvency challenges, making revenue considerations critical
4. **Distributional Effects**: Current taxation disproportionately affects middle- and upper-middle-income beneficiaries

### Research Questions

This analysis examines six different approaches to reforming Social Security benefits taxation:

1. **Complete Repeal**: Eliminating taxation of Social Security benefits entirely
2. **Flat Taxation**: Taxing 85% of all Social Security benefits regardless of income
3. **Flat Taxation with Senior Deduction Extension**: Combining flat taxation with permanent extension of the bonus senior deduction
4. **Tax Credit Approach**: Replacing the bonus senior deduction with a nonrefundable tax credit while implementing flat taxation
5. **Roth-Style Swap**: Taxing employer payroll contributions instead of Social Security benefits
6. **Phased Roth-Style Swap**: Gradually transitioning from benefit taxation to payroll contribution taxation

Each approach presents different trade-offs between revenue generation, administrative simplicity, distributional effects, and trust fund solvency. This analysis uses PolicyEngine's microsimulation capabilities to quantify the 10-year budgetary impacts of each reform option, providing policymakers with detailed estimates to inform their decision-making process.

### Methodology Overview

Our analysis employs PolicyEngine's microsimulation framework, which uses the Enhanced Current Population Survey (CPS) dataset to model the effects of policy changes on individual taxpayers and households. The microsimulation approach allows us to:

- Calculate precise revenue impacts for each reform option
- Analyze distributional effects across income groups
- Account for behavioral responses and interactions with other tax provisions
- Provide year-by-year estimates over a 10-year budget window (2026-2035)
