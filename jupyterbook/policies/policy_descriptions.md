# Policy Descriptions

This chapter provides detailed descriptions of the six Social Security taxation reform options analyzed in this study. Each policy represents a different approach to addressing the current system's complexity while balancing revenue needs, administrative simplicity, and distributional considerations.

## Policy 1: Full Repeal of Social Security Benefits Taxation

### Description
This option completely eliminates the taxation of Social Security benefits beginning in 2026. The bonus senior deduction expires at the end of 2028 as per current law.

### Key Features
- **Start Date**: 2026
- **Taxation Rate**: 0% for all Social Security benefits
- **Bonus Senior Deduction**: Expires at end of 2028 (current law)
- **Revenue Allocation**: Revenue loss allocated to OASDI and HI trust funds based on current law projections

### Implementation Details
This option eliminates all provisions related to Social Security benefits taxation, returning to the pre-1983 system where benefits were not subject to federal income tax.

### Key Characteristics
- **Revenue Impact**: Eliminates revenue from Social Security benefits taxation
- **Administrative Complexity**: Removes all Social Security taxation provisions
- **Distributional Effects**: Removes tax burden from Social Security beneficiaries
- **Trust Fund Impact**: Reduces revenue flow to Social Security trust funds

## Policy 2: Taxation of 85% of Social Security Benefits

### Description
This option simplifies the current two-tier system by taxing 85% of all Social Security benefits regardless of income level, beginning in 2026. The bonus senior deduction expires at the end of 2028.

### Key Features
- **Start Date**: 2026
- **Taxation Rate**: 85% of all Social Security benefits
- **Income Thresholds**: Eliminated (all benefits taxed at same rate)
- **Bonus Senior Deduction**: Expires at end of 2028 (current law)
- **Revenue Allocation**: Additional revenue allocated to OASDI and HI trust funds maintaining current projected shares

### Implementation Details
This approach replaces the current two-tier taxation system with a single flat rate applied to all Social Security benefits, regardless of the taxpayer's income level.

### Key Characteristics
- **Revenue Impact**: Changes revenue generation compared to current law
- **Administrative Complexity**: Reduces complexity by eliminating income thresholds
- **Distributional Effects**: Changes tax burden distribution across income groups
- **Trust Fund Impact**: Alters revenue flow to Social Security trust funds

## Policy 3: Taxation of 85% of Social Security Benefits with Permanent Bonus Senior Deduction Extension

### Description
This option combines the flat 85% taxation rate with a permanent extension of the bonus senior deduction beyond 2028.

### Key Features
- **Start Date**: 2026
- **Taxation Rate**: 85% of all Social Security benefits
- **Income Thresholds**: Eliminated
- **Bonus Senior Deduction**: Permanently extended past 2028
- **Revenue Allocation**: To be determined based on full budget estimates

### Implementation Details
This approach combines flat taxation of Social Security benefits with permanent extension of the bonus senior deduction, creating interaction effects between the two provisions.

### Key Characteristics
- **Revenue Impact**: Net effect depends on interaction between flat taxation and deduction extension
- **Administrative Complexity**: Moderate simplification of Social Security taxation
- **Distributional Effects**: Provides ongoing tax relief through permanent deduction
- **Trust Fund Impact**: Depends on net revenue effects after accounting for deduction costs

## Policy 4: Nonrefundable Tax Credit Approach

### Description
This option replaces the bonus senior deduction with a nonrefundable tax credit available to all Social Security beneficiaries, while implementing 85% taxation of benefits. The credit can only be applied against taxes owed on Social Security benefits.

### Key Features
- **Start Date**: 2026
- **Taxation Rate**: 85% of all Social Security benefits
- **Tax Credit**: Nonrefundable credit (amount to be determined)
- **Credit Application**: Only against taxes on Social Security benefits
- **Tax Calculation**: Social Security benefits treated as "last" income for marginal rate purposes
- **Revenue Allocation**: Additional revenue allocated to OASDI and HI trust funds

### Illustrative Example
An individual with $5,000 in Social Security income and $10,000 in other income:
- Taxable Social Security Income: $5,000 × 85% = $4,250
- Taxes on Social Security: $4,250 × 10% = $425
- Credit Received: MIN($425, $600) = $425
- Net Taxes: $925 - $500 = $425

### Implementation Details
This approach introduces a targeted tax credit mechanism specifically for Social Security beneficiaries while implementing flat taxation of benefits.

### Key Characteristics
- **Revenue Impact**: Depends on credit amount and interaction with flat taxation
- **Administrative Complexity**: Moderate simplification with new credit mechanism
- **Distributional Effects**: Provides targeted relief to Social Security beneficiaries
- **Trust Fund Impact**: Net effect depends on credit design

## Policy 5: Roth-Style Swap

### Description
This option fundamentally changes the timing of Social Security taxation by taxing employer payroll contributions instead of Social Security benefits, similar to Roth retirement accounts.

### Key Features
- **Start Date**: 2026
- **Employer Contributions**: All employer payroll contributions included in taxable income
- **Social Security Benefits**: Completely excluded from taxable income
- **Bonus Senior Deduction**: Expires at end of 2028 (current law)
- **Revenue Allocation**: 
  - OASDI contributions → OASDI trust funds
  - HI contributions → HI trust fund
  - Revenue loss from eliminated TOB allocated to maintain current trust fund shares

### Implementation Details
This approach shifts the timing of Social Security-related taxation from retirement to working years by taxing employer payroll contributions instead of benefits received.

### Key Characteristics
- **Revenue Impact**: Timing shift rather than net revenue change
- **Administrative Complexity**: Eliminates Social Security taxation complexity
- **Distributional Effects**: Shifts tax burden from retirees to workers
- **Trust Fund Impact**: Maintains revenue flow through different mechanism

## Policy 6: Phased Roth-Style Swap

### Description
This option implements the Roth-style swap gradually over time, phasing in employer payroll contribution taxation while phasing out Social Security benefits taxation.

### Key Features
- **Start Date**: 2026
- **Employer Contribution Phase-In**: 1 percentage point per year until full 7.65% is taxable
- **Benefits Taxation Phase-Out**: 5 percentage points per year starting in 2029
  - 2029: 45%/80% (base/additional rates)
  - 2030: 40%/75%
  - ...
  - 2038: 0%/35%
  - ...
  - 2045: 0%/0%
- **Bonus Senior Deduction**: Expires at end of 2028 (current law)
- **Revenue Allocation**: 
  - During phase-in: Revenue allocated based on contribution type
  - After phase-in: Same as Policy 5

### Implementation Details
This approach implements the Roth-style swap through a gradual transition period, allowing for adjustment over time rather than immediate implementation.

### Key Characteristics
- **Revenue Impact**: Gradual transition with minimal short-term disruption
- **Administrative Complexity**: Gradual simplification over time
- **Distributional Effects**: Gradual shift in tax burden timing
- **Trust Fund Impact**: Maintains revenue flow throughout transition
